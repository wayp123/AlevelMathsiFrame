Derivative explained(0001). (9979).

See the graph of a function, drawn in white, and a tangent line to that function, drawn in blue(0002). The slope of the tangent line, is equal to the derivative of the function, at the marked point(0003).

In calculus, a branch of mathematics, the derivative, is a measurement, of how a function changes, when the values of its inputs change(0004). Loosely speaking, a derivative can be thought of as, how much, or how fast, a quantity is changing, at some given point(0005). For example, the derivative of the position, or distance of a car, at some point in time, is the instantaneous velocity, or instantaneous speed _respectively_, at which that car is traveling, _conversely the integral of the velocity, is the cars position_(0006).

A closely related notion is the differential of a function(0007).

The derivative of a function, at a chosen input value, describes the best linear approximation of the function, near that input value(0008). For a real-valued function of a single real variable, the derivative at a point, equals the slope of the tangent line, to the graph of the function, at that point(0009). In higher dimensions, the derivative of a function at a point, is a linear transformation called the linearization, because the powers reduce and the tangent, is a linear, or straight line(0010).

The process of finding a derivative is called differentiation(0011). The fundamental theorem of calculus states that differentiation is the reverse process to integration(0012).  This is so, because differentiation involves division, since the slope is y over x, and integration involves multiplication, since the area under the curve is, y times x(0013).
