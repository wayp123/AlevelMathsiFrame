Linear Equations(0001).

 

Straight lines(0002).

 

A straight line has a fixed or constant gradient(0003). The gradient of a line, and its y, intercept are the two pieces of information that distinguish, one line from another line(0004).

 

b0002

 

Equations of a straight line(0005).

 

The most common form of a straight line is,

y = mx plus c(0006). m is the gradient of the line, and c is the point at which the line intercepts the y-axis, or where x=0(0007). When c is 0, the line passes through the origin 0,0(0008).

 

Other forms of the equation are:

x = A, used for vertical lines of an infinite gradient and

y = b is used for horizontal lines with a zero gradient(0009).

 

Also some equations are commonly written as

px plus Q y plus c = 0(0010).

 

 

b0003

 

Gradient of a line(0011).

 

The steepness of a line can be measured by its gradient, which is the change in the y direction, divided by the change in the x direction(0012). The letter m, is used to denote the gradient(0013).

 

The formula to find a gradient is:,

m= y2, minus y1 over, x2, minus x1(0014). 

 

As a side note

 

tan theta = m(0015).

 

 

b0004

 

Point-Gradient Form(0016).

The equation of a line having the point, or coordinate,

 x1, and y1, and having a gradient of m, is

y, minus y1 = m, x minus x1(0017). Then you can simply rearrange the equation into the form

y = mx plus c(0018).

 

 

b0005

 

Parallel lines(0019).

 

A pair of lines are parallel if their gradients are equal, or when,

m1=m2, and the symbol is (0020).

 

So, in order to find the equation of a parallel line, you need the slope of the original line, and one set of co-ordinates on the parallel line(0021). Then you use the Point-Gradient formula, to find the equation of the parallel line(0022).

 

 

b0006

Perpendicular lines(0023).

 

A pair of lines are perpendicular when, the product of their gradients is minus 1 or,

m1 times m2 = minus 1, and the symbol is (0024).

 

So if you need the equation of a line, perpendicular to another line, all you need to do is replace the gradient m, with the negative reciprocal of m(0025).

 

So for example, if line 1, is,

y = 2x plus 3, and you need to find the line perpendicular to this that goes through the point 0,1_(0026).

 

then the gradient of the new line is,

m = minus 1 over, 2(0027).

 

This gives,

y = minus x over, 2 plus c(0028).

1= minus 0 over, 2 plus c(0029).

 

which gives,

c = 1(0030).

so,

y=1 minus x over, 2(0031).

 

b0008

 

Distance between two points(0032).

 

Using the co-ordinates of two points, it is possible to calculate the distance between them, using Pythagoras theorem(0033).

 

The distance d, between any two points,

 

A, x1, y1, and B, x2, y2 is given by:(0034).

 

d= the square root of x2 minus x1 squared plus y2 minus y1 squared(0035).

 

 

b0009

 

The Mid-point of a line(0036).

 

When the coordinate of two points are known, the mid-point is the point halfway between those two points(0037).

 

For any two points,

 

Ax1, y1, and B, x2, y2, the co-ordinates of the mid-point of A, to B, can be found by(0038).

x1 plus x2 over, 2, and y1 plus y2 over, 2(0039).

 

 

b0010

 

Intersection of lines(0040).

 

Any two straight lines will meet at a point, as long as they are not parallel(0041). You can find the point of intersection simply by solving the two equations simultaneously(0042). This is also true for curves, although non-linear curves may intersect at multiple points, or not at all, and usually require different methods to solve(0043).
