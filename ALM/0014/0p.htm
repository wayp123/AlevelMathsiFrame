Curves of polynomials(0001).

 

It is possible to look at a polynomial, and get an idea of the general shape of its curve(0002). The most important factors are, the degree of the polynomial, and the sign of the coefficient, of the highest power(0003). For a more accurate sketch of the graph, you will need to know where the curve has turning points, and where it intersects the x, and y axis(0004). A sketch with this information will often be sufficiently detailed, and is usually much quicker, and easier than plotting the curve point by point(0005).

 

b0002 

 

Turning points_(0006).

 

A turning point, is where a curve, that is decreasing, changes to increasing, or vice versa(0007). On a graph, the curve simply, changes direction, hence the name turning point(0008). Dont confuse with points of inflection, see later(0009).

 

In_ y = x squared, the turning point is at  _0,0_, the trough of the bucket(0010). The turning point in y = x squared, is a minimum(0011). _plural minima_(0012). A maximum _plural maxima_ is the same bucket shape, but upside down(0013). For a polynomial of degree n, its curve will have at most, n minus 1 turning points(0014).

Behaviour with extreme values of x(0015).

 

What happens to a polynomial at large positive, and negative values for x?(0016).

 

If we say 1000 is our large number, we can put it into

6x4 plus 4x3 minus 15x2 minus 10x, we can take a look at what happens:(0017).

 

b0004

 

First for

 x = 1000, 6000000000000 plus 4000000000 minus 15000000 minus 10000 = 6003970000000(0018).

 

Now for

x = neg 1000, 6000000000000 minus 4000000000 minus 15000000 plus 10000 = 5995985010000(0019).

 

 

b0005

 

You can see, that the most change in the final answer is made by the x4 term(0020). This is the dominant term(0021). For a polynomial of degree n, xn is the dominant term as  x approaches infinity(0022).

 

Once you have investigated the behaiviour of polynomials, with extreme values of x, you may notice that if n, is even, it will share the same general shape as x squared, and if n, is odd it will share the same general shape as x cubed(0023).

 

 

b0006

If the coefficient of_ xn is positive, curves with an even value of n, will have a bucket shape(0024). This means that the curve is always positive, for large negative, and positive values of x(0025). Curves with an odd value of n, will generally be negative when x is negative, and positive when x is positive _like y = x_(0026).

 

If the coefficient of xn is negative, curves with an even value of n, will have an upside down bucket shape(0027). Curves with an odd value of n, will generally be positive when x is negative, and negative when x is positive _similar to y = neg x)(0028).

 

 

b0007

 

Intersections with axes(0029).

 

To find where the curve crosses the y axis, you can simply look at the constant term(0030).

y = x3 plus 2x2 minus 9x minus 18, for example, would cross the y axis at neg 18, or where x=0(0031). If there is no constant term, the curve will go through the origin, _0,0_, and would therfore cross the y axis at 0(0032).

 

In factorised form, you can find the points, at which the curve crosses the x axis, or where y=0(0033).

y = x plus 3, x plus 2, x minus 3, would cross the x axis at neg 3, neg 2, and 3, because by making one of the brackets equal to 0, it makes the whole polynomial equal to 0, and therefore y equal to 0(0034). y = 0, is the line of the x axis(0035). Each instance of the curve, crossing the x axis, is a root of the equation(0036). A polynomial of degree n, will have at most n, roots(0037).

 

b0008

 

To solve an equation means to find every root(0038). Polynomials of degree 2 can be solved using the quadratic formula(0039). For higher degree polynomials you may need to factorise the equation like above, plot the graph point by point, and look at where the curve crosses the x axis, or use a numerical method(0040). You can check your answers by substituting them into the original equation, and seeing if the result is 0(0041).
