Equations_(0001). (9980).

An equation consists of two expressions joined by the equals sign (0002). Everything on the left-hand side is equal to everything on the right-hand side(0003).

 

for example,

2 plus 3 = 4 plus 1(0004).

 

Some equations contain a variable, usually denoted by x or y , though any symbol can be used'(0005).

Manipulating expressions _Simplification_(0018).

 

Sometimes, expressions will be messier than they need to be, and they can be represented in an easier to understand form(0019). The skills here are essential to the rest of the A level course, although it is very likely that you have already covered them at GCSE(0020). Basic rules of simplification are, to multiply or add terms where possible, eliminate brackets and fractions, etc(0128).

 

b0004 

 

Collecting like terms_(0021).

 

When collecting like terms, you simply add all the terms in x together, all the terms in y together, and all the terms in z together(0022). The same applies for any other letter that represents a variable(0023).

 

b0005 

 

For example_,

2x plus 4y plus 8z minus 3x minus 7y minus 2z plus 4x_(0024).

 

becomes_,

2x minus 3x plus 4x = 3x, 4y minus 7y = minus 3y, 8z minus 2z = 6z(0025).

 

So, by adding all the answers,

2x plus 4y plus 8z minus 3x minus 7y minus 2z plus 4x(0026).

 

simplifies as,

3x minus 3y plus 6z(0027).

 

b0006 

 

Multiplication_(0028).

 

Multiplication of different variables such as,   A, times B, becomes  A, B(0029). Multiplication of single variables become, indices, so  x times x becomes  x squared(0030).

 

Like addition, and subtraction, when you multiply, you can keep like terms together(0031).

 

So for example

2x squared Z, times 3 y z squared, times 4 x y cubed(0032).

 

becomes_:,

24 x 3, y 4, z 3(0033).

 

b0007 

 

Fractions_(0034).

 

It is quite often that fractions are encountered(0035). Therefore we need to learn how to handle them properly(0036). When working with fractions, the rule is to make all of the denominators equal, and then write the expression as one fraction(0037).You need to multiply both the top, and bottom, by the same amount, which is equivalent to 1, to keep the value of the fraction the same(0038).

 

b0008 

 

For example,

3x over, 2, plus 2y over, 5, minus z over, 10(0039).

The common denomimator is 10, with factors 2, and 5(0040).

 

Multiply the 1st term by 5 over, 5, or 1, to change the factor 2, into 10(0041).

 

Multiply the 2nd term by 2 over, 2, or 1, to change the factor 5, into 10(0042).

 

Leave the 3rd term as it is, it is the same as multiplying by 1, since it is already 10(0043).

 

b0009 

 

You now have,

15x over, 10 plus 4 y over, 10 minus z over, 10(0044).

 

which becomes,

15x plus 4y minus z over, 10(0045).

 

b0010 

 

Solving equations(0046).

 

Often to solve an equation you must rearrange it so that the unknown term is on its own side of the equals sign(0047).

 

By rearranging,

2 plus x = 5 to, x = 5 minus 2(0048).

 

x has been made the subject of the equation(0049). Now by simplifying the equation, you can find that the solution is, x = 3__(0050).

 

 

b0011 

 

An equation with a variable, will only hold true, for certain values of that variable(0051). For example_ 2 plus x = 5 is only true for x = 3_(0052). The values that the variables have, when the equation is true, are called the solutions of the equation(0053). Therefore x = 3, is the solution of the equation, 2 plus x = 5_(0054).

 

b0012 

 

Changing the subject of an equation(0055).

 

You will usually be given equations that are more complex than the example above(0056). To move a term, from one side of the equals sign to the other, you have to do the same thing on both sides of the equals sign(0057).

 

b0013 

 

For example, to make x the subject of,

y=4A x squared plus b over 3(0058).

 

Multiply both sides by 3 to eliminate the fraction(0059).

 

to get_

3y = 4A, x squared plusb(0060).

 

then divide both sides by 4 A, to get the x term alone(0061).

 

gives,

3y over, 4A, = x squared plusb(0062).

 

then Subtract B from both sides to seperate the x term(0063).

 

b0014

 

3y over, 4A, minus b = x squared'(0064).

 

then Square root both sides to remove the x squared(0065).

 

we get,

 

Root 3y over, 4A, minus b = x(0066).

Solving quadratic equations(0067).

 

Quadratic equations, are equations, where the variable is raised to the power of 2, and unlike linear equations, there are a possible, two roots(0068). A root is one value of the variable, where the equation is true, and to fully solve an equation, you must find all of the roots(0069). For a quadratic equation you can factorize it, and then easily find which values make the equation valid(0070). The example above is quite a simple case(0071). You will usually be given a more complicated equation(0072).

 

b0016 

 

such as

2x squared plus 5x plus 3 = 0_(0073).

 

If the equation isnt already in the form,

A, x squared plus bx plus c = 0, rearrange it so that it is(0074).

 

The steps needed to factorize,

2x squared plus 5x plus 3 = 0 are(0075).

 

multiply the coefficient of x squared by the constant term, to give,

2, times 3,=6(0076).

 

b0017 

 

Find two numbers that add, to give 5 {coefficient of x}, and also multiply to give 6 {answer from the previous step}, so,

2 times 3 = 6, and 2 plus 3 = 5 (0077).

 

Split,

5x to 2x plus 3x {from the results of the previous step_(0078).

 

giving,

2x squared plus 2x plus 3x plus 3 =0(0079).

 

Simplify, by using, or taking out the factors of 2, and then 3, and factorize, to get,

2x, by x plus1, plus 3, by xplus1=0(0080).

 

b0018 

 

Simplify further to,

2x plus 3 by x plus1=0(0081).

 

So,

2x plus 3 times x plus 1 = 0, is 2x squared plus 5x plus 3 = 0, in factorized form(0082).

 

You can now use the fact that, any number multiplied by 0, is 0, to find the roots of the equation(0083). The numbers, that make each bracket equal to 0, are the roots of the equation(0084). In the example, the roots are, minus 1.5, and minus 1(0085).

 

It is also possible to solve a quadratic equation using the quadratic formula, or by completing the square(0086). see later(0087).

 

 

b0019 

 

Simultaneous equations_(0088).

 

Simultaneous equations are useful in solving, two or more variables, at once(0089). Basic simultaneous equations consist of two linear expressions, and can be solved by three different methods, elimination, substitution, or by plotting the graph(0090).

 

b0020 

 

Elimination method_(0091).

 

The basic principle of the elimination method is to manipulate one, or more of the expressions in order to cancel out, or eliminate, one of the variables, and then solve for the correct solution(0092).

 

b0021 

 

An example of this

2x plus 3y = 10, _Assigning the number _1_ to this equation_, 2x plus 6y = 6, _Assigning the number _2_ to this equation(0093).

 

From this, we can see that by multiplying equation _1_, by a factor of 2, and then subtracting equation _2_ from this new equation , the y-variable will be eliminated(0094).

 

_1_ times 2 becomes 4x plus 6y=20, and equation 1, becomes 1a(0095).

 

Now subtracting _2_, from _1a_ we get,

2x, plus0y=14(0096).

 

b0022 

 

Now that we have eliminated y, we get  2x = 14, so we can solve for x, which in this case is 7(0097).

 

Substitute the newly found x into _1_, we get

14 plus 3y = 10 (0098).

 

And we find that y = neg 4 over, 3_(0099).

 

So, the solution to the two equations _1_ and _2_ are

x = 7, and y = neg 4 over, 3 (0100).

 

b0024 

 

Substitution method_(0101).

 

The substitution method relies on being able to rearrange the expressions to isolate a single variable, in the form variable = expression(0102). From this result this new expression can then be substituted for the variable itself, and the solutions evaluated(0103).

 

b0025 

 

An example of this:,

2x plus 3y = 12, _Assigning the number _1_ to this equation_ x plus y = 6, _Assigning the number _2_ to this equation(0104).

 

From this expression, it is possible to see that _2_ is the most simplistic expression, and thus will be the better choice to rearrange(0105).

 

Taking _2_, and rearranging this into,

x = 6 minus y, to become 2a(0106).

 

Subbing _2a_ into _1_, we get,

2 times _6 minus y_ plus 3y = 12(0107).

 

b0026 

 

Solving this, we get that y = 0(0108).

 

Again we can sub this result into one of the original equations, to solve for x(0109). In this case_ x = 6 (0110).

 

Note that for situations, in which one of the equations is non-linear, you must isolate one variable in the linear equation, and substitute it into the non-linear one(0111). Then you can solve the quadratic equation with one of the methods above(0112).

 

Another form of substitution is, if youve got a similar expression in both equations, like in this case,

2x plus 3y = 10 Assigning the number _1_ to this equation 2x plus 6y = 6 Assigning the number _2_ to this equation(0113).

 

b0027 

 

Here, 2x is found in both equations, so rearranging we get:,

 

2x = 10 minus 3y, 2x = 6 minus 6y(0114).

 

And since,

2x = 2x, you could make equation 1=2(0115).

 

then,

10 minus 3y = 6 minus 6y(0116).

 

b0028 

 

which gives y = minus 4 over, 3(0117).

 

Now youve got y, finding x will be the same as above(0118).

Solving problems with simultaneous equations(0119).

 

Often, you will be given problems which you must be able to write out as a pair of simultaneous equations(0120). You will need to recognize such problems, and write them out correctly before solving them(0121). Most problems will be similar to these examples with some differences(0122).

 

b0030 

 

Example(0123).

 

At a record store, 2 albums, and 1 single costs 10 pounds(0124). 1 album, and 2 singles cost 8 pounds(0125). Find the cost of an album, and the cost of a single(0126).

 

Taking an album as A, and a single as s, the two equations would be:,

2A plus s = 10, and A, plus 2 s, = 8(0127).
