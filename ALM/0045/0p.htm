Sequences and Series(0001).

Definitions(0002).

 

A_ sequence_, is simply a list of numbers, in a particular order(0003). We call these numbers, the terms_, of the sequence(0004). For instance, 2, 4, 6, 8, are the first four terms, in the sequence, of even positive integers(0005).

 

When we take the sum, of the terms, in a sequence, we get a series_(0006).

For example_ 2 plus 4 plus 6 plus 8 plus, is a series__(0007).

We denote the terms, in a sequence, by tn where n is the number of the term, in question(0008).

For example, we have  T1 = 2, T2 = 4, T3 = 6, and so on, in the sequence described above(0009).

 

A__definition, is a rule, that tells us how to compute, each term, in a sequence(0010).

For example, a rule for the sequence above is, tn=2n(0011).

 

_A_ relation_, describes how each term, is related to other terms(0012).

For instance, a relation, for the above sequence is,

T, n plus 1, = Tn, plus 2(0013).

 

b0003

 

Sigma_ Notation_(0014).

 

As you might have suspected, describing a series, with the help of some of its terms, isnt always a good idea, if too few terms are used, the series can be ambiguous to your reader; on the other hand, you risk insulting your reader, by writing out too many terms!(0015). To express a series succinctly, we use the sigma notation instead_(0016).

 

b0004

In general, a series may be written as,

the sum of all terms of F, k, beginning with F, k0 up to and including Fn_(0017).

 

Hence_,

the sum of all terms of F, k, beginning with f, k0 up to and including fN =, f k0 plus fk0 plus 1 plus f k0 plus 2 plus plus fn(0018).

 

 

As an example, the series  2 plus 4 plus 6 plus 8 plus, may be written, from its term definition as,

the sum of all terms, 2k, from k = 1, to k = infinity(0019).

Recognising Simple Progressions(0020).

 

A_ _progression_, is just another word for a sequence_(0021). In this module, you are expected to be well acquainted, with two, very common types of progressions, the arithmetic progression__, and the_ geometric progression__(0022).

 

Briefly, an arithmetic progression, or AP_, is a sequence, in which each successive term _not sum of terms_ is the sum of the previous term, with a fixed value(0023).

An example, of an AP is, 1, 4, 7, 10, where the difference, between successive terms, is 3(0024).

 

A geometric progression, or GP_, is a sequence, in which each successive term is, the product of the previous term, with a fixed value(0025).

An example of a GP is 2, 4, 8, 16, 32, where each term, is twice the value, of the previous term(0026).

 

b0006

 

Arithmetic Progression_ (AP)(0027).

 

An arithmetic progression _AP_ is a sequence, that can be written in the following way(0028).

 

A, A plus d, A plus 2d, A plus 3d, A plus 4d(0029).

Where a and d are constants(0030).

 

The first term,  t1 in the AP, is denoted by A, and the common difference_, between subsequent terms, is denoted by d(0031).

 

Thus, the series 1, 4, 7, 10, is an arithmetic progression, with A, = 1, and d = 3(0032).

 

b0007

 

Rules(0033).

 

The common difference, d can be calculated by(0034).

d, = Tn, minus, T, n minus 1, where n,= 2, 3, 4(0035).

 

The nth term, is given by,

Tn, = A, plus n minus 1, d (0036).

 

The sum, of the first n terms, of an AP, with T 1, as its first term, and Tn, as its last term, is given by(0037).

Sn, = n over 2, t1 plus tn,= n over 2, 2A, plus n minus 1, d(0038).

 

b0008

 

In fact, more generally, the sum of, n consecutive terms, in an AP is given by,

the number of terms, over 2, first term, plus last term(0039).

 

Example(0040).

 

What is the sum of the even numbers 2, 4, 6, 8, upto, 100?(0041).

 

The number of terms = 50, so,

S50, = 50 over 2, 2 plus 100, = 50 over 2, 2 2, plus 50 minus 1, 2, = 2550(0042).

 

The given sequence, can also be expressed as an AP, with T1, = A, = 2, and d, = 2 (0043).

We want the sum, of the first 50 terms, of the AP(0044).
